ECON1626 · AI Future Forecast · Australia · 2027–2030

Australia stands to gain $142 billion — but only if policy keeps pace with AI.

Artificial intelligence is already reshaping Australia's labour market, productivity trajectory, and industrial structure. This forecast argues that by 2030, AI could add up to $142 billion annually to Australia's GDP — but the distribution of those gains, and the fate of up to 1.3 million workers facing role transitions, hinges critically on the speed and quality of government and industry response. These projections carry genuine uncertainty: capability timelines, firm adoption rates, and structural labour market shifts are all difficult to pin down. This essay surfaces those gaps explicitly throughout.

02 — Capabilities → Economic Channels

How AI translates into economic outcomes

AI capabilities — from large language models to robotic process automation — connect to macroeconomic outcomes through four primary channels. Each mechanism carries both opportunity and disruption depending on how firms and policymakers respond.

⚡

Productivity augmentation

AI tools augment worker output — drafting, analysis, coding, customer service — lifting output per hour without necessarily reducing headcount. McKinsey estimates 0.2–4.1% annual productivity gains are achievable.

🔄

Task automation & displacement

Routine cognitive tasks — data entry, document processing, basic legal drafting — face high substitution risk. Jobs and Skills Australia finds automation potential is concentrated in structured, predictable roles.

🏭

Industry restructuring

AI enables new business models and lowers entry barriers in knowledge-intensive industries. Healthcare, education, and professional services face structural change, not just efficiency gains.

📊

Distribution of gains

Without intervention, productivity gains may concentrate among high-skill workers and capital owners. Australia's AI Opportunities Report projects women could see 35% higher wage growth due to their representation in AI-boosted sectors.

The critical insight is that augmentation — not replacement — dominates in the near term. Jobs and Skills Australia found current generative AI is more likely to enhance workers' efforts than eliminate roles outright, but this balance shifts significantly post-2026 as multimodal and agentic AI systems mature. The inflection point is not a cliff — it is a slope, and Australia's policy response must be calibrated to the gradient.

⚠ Uncertainty note: Sector exposure estimates measure potential exposure, not guaranteed displacement. Actual outcomes depend on firm-level adoption decisions, wage bargaining, and regulatory settings — all of which remain fluid. See the Uncertainty section for a full treatment of model limits.
