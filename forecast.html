ECON1626 · AI Future Forecast · Australia · 2027–2030

Australia stands to gain $142 billion — but only if policy keeps pace with AI.

The numbers around AI and Australia's economy are striking — up to $142 billion in annual GDP gains by 2030, according to the Business Council of Australia. But headline figures can be misleading. Who captures those gains, and what happens to the 1.3 million workers likely facing significant role changes, depends almost entirely on policy choices made between now and 2028. This essay maps those choices — and is honest about what we don't know yet.

02 — Capabilities → Economic Channels

How AI translates into economic outcomes

AI doesn't affect the economy through one clean channel — it works through several at once, pulling in different directions. Understanding which dominates, and for whom, is the starting point for any credible forecast.

⚡

Productivity augmentation

In many knowledge-intensive roles, AI acts more like a capable assistant than a replacement — speeding up drafting, analysis, and customer interactions. McKinsey puts the productivity gain range at 0.2–4.1% annually, a wide band reflecting genuine uncertainty about adoption pace.

🔄

Task automation & displacement

Not all workers will be augmented. Roles built around repetitive cognitive tasks — data entry, document processing, routine legal work — face real substitution risk. Jobs and Skills Australia finds this risk is concentrated in structured, predictable roles rather than spread evenly.

🏭

Industry restructuring

Beyond individual jobs, AI is reshaping how entire industries are organised. In healthcare, education, and professional services, the shift isn't just about doing existing work faster — it's about what gets done at all, and by whom.

📊

Distribution of gains

Left unmanaged, productivity gains tend to flow to capital owners and high-skill workers. Interestingly, Australia's AI Opportunities Report suggests women may see 35% higher wage growth — concentrated in sectors where AI augments rather than replaces.

The evidence so far points to an augmentation phase, not a replacement phase — at least for now. Jobs and Skills Australia found current generative AI tools are more likely to make workers more productive than to eliminate their roles. But that calculus shifts after 2026 as more capable agentic systems come online. The window for getting policy right is narrow.

⚠ Uncertainty note: These sector exposure figures come from task-mapping models, not direct firm surveys — so they show potential exposure, not guaranteed outcomes. How firms actually respond, what gets bargained in enterprise agreements, and how regulators move will all shape the real numbers considerably.

03 — Scenarios

Three futures for Australia by 2030

Where Australia ends up by 2030 isn't fixed — it depends on choices being made right now by governments, firms, and workers. The three scenarios below aren't predictions; they're structured ways of thinking about which future becomes more or less likely depending on those choices.

Managed Transition

The most likely path given where things currently stand. AI adoption spreads steadily, the National AI Plan holds together, and reskilling programs expand — though they lag behind the pace of change. Productivity reaches around 1.8% annually by 2028, and while displacement is real, new job creation gradually offsets it over a 5–7 year window.

  • GDP contribution by 2030$80–100B
  • Annual productivity growth (2028)+1.8%
  • Workers needing transition~900K
  • Wage inequality (Gini change)+0.02
  • AI adoption rate (SMEs)~35%

Accelerated Prosperity

In this scenario, Australia actually gets it right. Front-loaded investment in compute, a reskilling system that moves at the speed of industry, and regulation that doesn't strangle innovation. Productivity pushes past 3% annually and gains spread more broadly — women, regional workers, and healthcare in particular. Ambitious, but the foundations exist if the political will follows.

  • GDP contribution by 2030$130–142B
  • Annual productivity growth (2028)+3.1%
  • Workers needing transition~600K (supported)
  • Wage inequality (Gini change)−0.01
  • AI adoption rate (SMEs)~65%

Disruption Without Safety Nets

The technology moves faster than the institutions meant to manage it. Large firms automate quickly, SMEs are left behind, and reskilling programs are underfunded. Administrative, legal, and financial roles disappear faster than new ones appear. Youth unemployment climbs, regional communities are hit hardest, and the gains from AI flow almost entirely to capital owners. No catastrophe required — just policy drift.

  • GDP contribution by 2030$40–60B
  • Annual productivity growth (2028)+0.9%
  • Workers needing transition~1.3M (unsupported)
  • Wage inequality (Gini change)+0.06
  • AI adoption rate (SMEs)~18%

04 — Policy Playbook

What Australia must do — and when

The December 2025 National AI Plan is a genuine step forward — but a plan is only as good as its implementation. What follows is a sequenced view of what needs to happen, who needs to do it, and what gets traded off along the way.

Immediate (2025–2026)

Skills & Reskilling Pipeline

Led by: DISER, Jobs and Skills Australia, state TAFE networks, unions (ACTU)

  • Scale AI Adopt Centres to all states and territories (DISER-funded)
  • Fund TAFE micro-credentials in AI-adjacent skills; target displaced admin workers first
  • Expand Jobs and Skills Australia's real-time occupation exposure monitoring
  • Mandate employer AI disclosure in enterprise agreements (Fair Work Commission)

KPIs: 50K workers reskilled by end 2026; 60% of SMEs aware of AI Adopt Centres.

Risk: Slow programs miss the displacement wave → front-load 2025 MYEFO funding; co-design curricula with industry.

Near-term (2026–2027)

Regulatory Framework

Led by: AI Safety Institute (AISI), OAIC, ACCC, Attorney-General's Department

  • Operationalise the AI Safety Institute with sector-specific risk mandates
  • Introduce risk-tiered AI regulation aligned with EU AI Act but tailored for Australian SMEs
  • Resolve copyright/text-and-data-mining impasse (Attorney-General's Dept + creative unions)
  • Require algorithmic transparency statements for high-stakes AI decisions (OAIC oversight)

KPIs: Risk-tiered framework legislated by mid-2027; AISI publishing quarterly assessments.

Risk: Over-regulation chills investment → use regulatory sandboxes; consult industry before finalising rules.

Medium-term (2027–2028)

Sovereign AI Capability

Led by: DISER, CSIRO, ARC, Defence (AUKUS pillar II), state governments

  • Fund domestic compute via public–private partnerships (NEXTDC, Macquarie data centres)
  • Develop Australian-trained foundational models for healthcare and public services (CSIRO)
  • Position Australia as regional AI exporter to Southeast Asia under Indo-Pacific framework
  • Expand ARC linkage grants for university–industry AI research pipelines

KPIs: $2B sovereign compute committed by 2027; 3 regional AI export agreements signed.

Trade-off: Expensive → prioritise shared infrastructure; benchmark against targeted imports.

Ongoing

Distributional Safeguards

Led by: Fair Work Commission, Treasury, DSS, Regional Development Australia

  • Link productivity gains to wage floors via Fair Work Commission modern award reviews
  • Target regional AI access programs through Regional Development Australia network
  • Treasury to publish annual AI distributional impact report (gender, region, sector)
  • DSS to reform JobSeeker with longer transition payment windows for AI-displaced workers

KPIs: Gini stable or improving by 2028; regional AI access gap narrowing year-on-year.

Trade-off: Redistribution may slow adoption → pair with productivity-sharing agreements, not pure taxation.

05 — Uncertainty & Limitations

What we don't know — and why it matters

Any honest forecast about AI needs to be upfront about what it doesn't know. The ranges in this essay are wide for good reason — here's a breakdown of where the uncertainty is highest, and where we're on slightly firmer ground.

High uncertainty

Pace of AI capability jumps

Every scenario here assumes steady capability improvements. But the jump from GPT-3 to GPT-4 caught most economists off guard — another step-change by 2026 would scramble any forecast built today.

High uncertainty

Firm adoption behaviour

McKinsey's midpoint requires 27% of tasks to be automated by 2030. Australian SMEs adopt technology slowly — capital constraints, risk aversion, and skills gaps all get in the way, and this gap may prove persistent.

Medium uncertainty

Labour market adjustment speed

Economic models predict new jobs emerge to replace displaced ones — and historically they have. But Australia's post-manufacturing experience shows this can take closer to a decade than a few years, especially in regional areas.

Medium uncertainty

Geopolitical AI dynamics

Australia doesn't control the US–China AI race or the export controls that come with it. AUKUS tech pillar arrangements offer some protection, but access to frontier compute and models remains partially outside Australia's hands.

Lower uncertainty

Direction of productivity impact

Whatever the size of the effect, virtually every serious analysis agrees AI will raise productivity. The argument is about how much and for whom — not whether the sign is positive. That's actually a useful anchor for policy planning.

Lower uncertainty

High-exposure sectors

There's strong cross-methodology agreement that finance, legal support, and administrative roles are most exposed. Healthcare and education show up as high-exposure too, but the evidence there points more toward augmentation than outright replacement.
